\chapter{Methodology}

\section{Introduction}

The microstructural evolution in our system is governed by the Cahn--Hilliard equation~[3.1] and the Allen--Cahn equation~[3.2]:
\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial c}{\partial t}=\nabla\cdot(M\nabla\mu)
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial \eta}{\partial t}=-L\frac{\delta F}{\delta \eta},
\end{equation}
where $M$ is the atomic mobility, $\mu$ is the chemical potential, and $L$ is the relaxation coefficient for the order parameter.

Chemical potential $\mu$ is defined as the variational derivative of the total free energy per atom, $F$, with respect to the local composition $c$,
\begin{equation}
\mu=\frac{\delta \left(F/N_v\right)}{\delta c}.
\end{equation}

The total free energy of the system $F$ is assumed to be given by the sum of a chemical contribution $F^{ch}$ and an elastic contribution $F^{el}$:
\begin{equation}
F=F^{ch}+F^{el}.
\end{equation}

The chemical contribution $F^{ch}$ is given by the following functional:
\begin{equation}
F^{ch}=N_v\int_\Omega \left[f(c,\eta)+K_c(\nabla c)^2+K_\eta(\nabla \eta)^2\right] d\Omega,
\end{equation}
where $f(c,\eta)$ is the bulk free energy per atom, $K_c$ and $K_\eta$ are the gradient energy coefficients for gradients in composition and order parameter, respectively, and $NV$ is the number of atoms per unit volume.

The bulk free energy density $f(c,\eta)$ is given by,
\begin{equation}
f(c,\eta)=f^{m}(c)\left(1-W(\eta)\right)+f^p(c)W(\eta)+P\eta^2(1-\eta)^2,
\end{equation}
where $P$, a constant, sets the height of the free energy barrier between the m-phase and p-phase.

In Eq.~(3.6), $f^{m}(c)$ and $f^p(c)$ stand for free energies of m (matrix) and p (precipitate) phases, respectively. In our work, they are assumed to take the following simple forms:
\begin{equation}
f^{m}(c)=Ac^2,
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
f^p(c)=B(1-c)^2,
\end{equation}
with positive constants $A$ and $B$. In Eq.~(3.6), $W(\eta)$ is an interpolation function~[15], given by:
\begin{equation}
W(\eta)=
\begin{cases}
0, & \text{for } \eta<0, \\
\eta^3(10-15\eta+6\eta^2), & \text{for } 0\leq \eta\leq 1, \\
1, & \text{for } \eta>1.
\end{cases}
\end{equation}

The elastic contribution to the free energy is:
\begin{equation}
F^{el}=\frac{1}{2}\int_\Omega \sigma_{ij}^{el}\epsilon_{ij}^{el}\,d\Omega,
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation}
\epsilon_{ij}^{el}=\epsilon_{ij}-\epsilon_{ij}^{0},
\end{equation}
where $\sigma_{ij}^{el}$ and $\epsilon_{ij}^{el}$ are elastic stress and strain tensors, respectively, and $\epsilon^0$ is the position-dependent eigenstrain (misfit strain). The total strain $\epsilon_{ij}$ is:
\begin{equation}
\epsilon_{ij}=\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{\partial u_i}{\partial r_j}+\frac{\partial u_j}{\partial r_i}\right),
\end{equation}
where $u_i$ is the displacement field.

Assuming both the m and p phases to be linearly elastic, we have:
\begin{equation}
\sigma_{kl}^{el}=C_{ijkl}\epsilon_{ij}^{el},
\end{equation}
where $C_{ijkl}$ is the elastic modulus tensor.

The stress field $\sigma_{ij}^{el}$ obeys the equation of mechanical equilibrium:
\begin{equation}
\sigma_{ij,j}^{el}=0.
\end{equation}

The eigenstrain $\epsilon_{ij}^{0}$ and the elastic moduli $C_{ijkl}$ are assumed to depend on the order parameter as follows:
\begin{equation}
\epsilon_{ij}^{0}(\eta)=\beta(\eta)e_T\delta_{ij},
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
C_{ijkl}(\eta)=C_{ijkl}^{eff}+\phi(\eta)\Delta C_{ijkl},
\end{equation}
where $e_T$ is a constant that determines the strength of the eigenstrain, $\delta_{ij}$ is the Kronecker delta, $\beta(\eta)$ and $\phi(\eta)$ are scalar interpolation functions, $C_{ijkl}^{eff}$ is an ``effective'' modulus, $C_{ijkl}^p$ and $C_{ijkl}^m$ are the elastic moduli tensor of the p and m phases, respectively, and $\Delta C_{ijkl}=C_{ijkl}^p-C_{ijkl}^m$. \section{Cahn Hilliard equation} 
The Cahn-Hilliard equation from Eq.~(3.1) can be rewritten as:
\[
\frac{\partial c}{\partial t}=\nabla\cdot\bigl(M\nabla\mu\bigr)
\]
where $\mu$ from Eq.~(3.3) is given by:
\[
\mu=\frac{\delta (F/N_v)}{\delta c}.
\]
From Eq.~(3.4), we have:
\[
F=F^{ch}+F^{el}.
\]
so, we can write $\mu$ as:
\[
\mu=\frac{\delta (F^{ch}/N_v)}{\delta c}+\frac{\delta (F^{el}/N_v)}{\delta c}.
\]
We get $\mu^{ch}$ by taking the functional derivative of $F^{ch}$, which is given by Eq.~(3.5). First we substitute Eq.~(3.7) and Eq.~(3.8) in Eq.~(3.6) and then into the Eq.~(3.5) expression for $\mu^{ch}$, we get:
\[
\mu^{ch}=2Ac(1-W(\eta))+2B(1-c)W(\eta)-2K_c\nabla^2.
\]
The elastic contribution to the total free energy is given by Eq.~(3.10):
\[
F^{el}=\frac{1}{2}\int_\Omega \sigma_{ij}^{el}\epsilon_{ij}^{el}\,d\Omega.
\]
To solve the Elastic contribution we have to first solve the Eq.~(3.11) and Eq.~(3.13) for the elastic stress($\sigma_{ij}^{el}$) and strain tensors($\epsilon_{ij}^{el}$), which are given by:
\[
\epsilon_{ij}^{el}=\epsilon_{ij}-\epsilon_{ij}^{0},
\]
\[\sigma_{kl}^{el}=C_{ijkl}\epsilon_{ij}^{el},
\]
For Elastic Strain we have Eq.~(3.12) and it suggests that we can get the total strain $\epsilon_{ij}$ by solving for the displacement field $u_i$ using the following equation:
\[\epsilon_{ij}=\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{\partial u_i}{\partial r_j}+\frac{\partial u_j}{\partial r_i}\right).    
\]
Also, we have $\epsilon_{ij}^{0}$ from Eq.~(3.15) and $C_{ijkl}$ from Eq.~(3.16). After substituting these into the expression for $\sigma_{ij}^{el}$, we can then solve for the elastic stress field by solving the equation of mechanical equilibrium given by Eq.~(3.14):
\[\epsilon_{ij}^{0}(\eta)=\beta(\eta)e_T\delta_{ij},
\]
\[C_{ijkl}(\eta)=C_{ijkl}^{eff}+\phi(\eta)\Delta C_{ijkl},
\]
\[\sigma_{ij,j}^{el}=0.
\]
After solving for $\sigma_{ij}^{el}$ and $\epsilon_{ij}^{el}$, we can substitute them into the expression for $F^{el}$ and then take the functional derivative to get $\mu^{el}$, which is the elastic contribution to the chemical potential. Finally, we can substitute $\mu^{ch}$ and $\mu^{el}$ into the Cahn-Hilliard equation to get the final form of the Cahn-Hilliard equation with elasticity. The final form of the Cahn-Hilliard equation with elasticity is given by:
\[
\frac{\partial c}{\partial t}=\nabla\cdot\bigl(M\nabla(\mu^{ch}+\mu^{el}))\bigr).
\]
\section{Allen-Cahn equation}
The Allen--Cahn equation from Eq.~(3.2) can be written as:
\[
\frac{\partial \eta}{\partial t} = -L\frac{\delta F}{\delta \eta}.
\]
Using $F=F^{ch}+F^{el}$ we write
\[
\frac{\delta F}{\delta \eta}=\frac{\delta F^{ch}}{\delta \eta}+\frac{\delta F^{el}}{\delta \eta}.
\]
In the absence of elasticity $\delta F^{el}/\delta\eta=0$, hence
\[
\frac{\delta (F^{ch}/N_v)}{\delta \eta}=\frac{\partial f(c,\eta)}{\partial \eta}-2K_{\eta}\nabla^2\eta.
\]
Using the bulk free energy density from Eq.~(3.6) we obtain the explicit chemical contribution
\[
\frac{\delta (F^{ch}/N_v)}{\delta \eta}=(f^p(c)-f^m(c))W'(\eta)+2P\eta(1-\eta)(1-2\eta)-2K_{\eta}\nabla^2\eta,
\]
where $W'(\eta)=dW/d\eta$. Substituting $f^m(c)=Ac^2$ and $f^p(c)=B(1-c)^2$ gives the commonly used form:
\[
\frac{\partial \eta}{\partial t} = -L\Big[(B(1-c)^2-Ac^2)W'(\eta)+2P\eta(1-\eta)(1-2\eta)-2K_{\eta}\nabla^2\eta\Big] + \frac{\delta F^{el}}{\delta \eta}.
\]
For the Allen--Cahn equation, the elastic contribution $\delta F^{el}/\delta \eta$ is solved in the same way as described in the previous section for the Cahn-Hilliard equation. After solving for $\delta F^{el}/\delta \eta$, we can substitute it into the above equation to get the final form of the Allen--Cahn equation with elasticity.

\subsection{Solving Elasticity}
The elastic moduli and the eigenstrains are non-periodic on $\Omega$ (since they depend on the composition field which is non-periodic on $\Omega$); therefore, the solution to the equilibrium equation (Eq.~(3.14)) should be such that it gives rise to a strain field $e(\mathbf{r})$ that is non-periodic on $\Omega$ (up to an additive constant). The displacement field $\mathbf{u}(\mathbf{r})$ which gives rise to such non-periodic strain fields can always be written as follows~[30]:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{u}=\mathbf{E}\,\mathbf{r}+\mathbf{u}^{\star} \label{eq:disp_decomp}
\end{equation}

where $\mathbf{u}^{\star}$ is a displacement field that is non-periodic on $\Omega$ and $\mathbf{E}$ is a constant, homogeneous strain tensor (assumed to be symmetric without loss of generality, since the antisymmetric part corresponds to a rigid rotation of the cell). It can be shown~[30] that $\mathbf{E}$ denotes the mean strain tensor of the cell:
\begin{equation}
\langle \{e_{ij}\}\rangle = E_{ij}
\end{equation}

with the following definition for the mean $\langle\{\cdot\}\rangle$ of a quantity $\{\cdot\}$:
\begin{equation}
\langle\{\cdot\}\rangle=\frac{1}{V}\int_\Omega \{\cdot\}\,d\Omega
\end{equation}

where $V$ is the volume of the representative domain $\Omega$. If we denote the periodic strain by $\epsilon^\star$ (such that $\langle \epsilon^\star\rangle=0$), the strain that we derive from the displacement decomposition (Eq.~\eqref{eq:disp_decomp}) becomes

\begin{equation}
\epsilon_{ij}=E_{ij}+\epsilon^{\star}_{ij}
\end{equation}

where

\begin{equation}
\epsilon^{\star}_{ij}=\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{\partial u^{\star}_i}{\partial r_j}+\frac{\partial u^{\star}_j}{\partial r_i}\right).
\end{equation}

Analogous to the expression Eq.~(3.18) for the homogeneous strain, we can define the mean stress on the domain $\Omega$ as follows:

\begin{equation}
\langle\{\sigma^{el}_{ij}\}\rangle=\frac{1}{V}\int_\Omega \sigma^{el}_{ij}\,d\Omega
\end{equation}

The mean stress thus calculated should equal the applied stress $\sigma^A$~[30,31,37] [As a brief aside,we note that while Refs.[30,31] used the homogenization assumption, Jin etal.[37] used a variational approach to arrive at the above conclusion, viz., that the mean stress should equal the applied stress]. Substituting for $\sigma^{el}_{ij}$ from Eq.~(3.13), we obtain
\begin{equation}
\sigma^A_{ij}=\frac{1}{V}\int_\Omega C_{ijkl}\left(E_{kl}+\epsilon^{\star}_{kl}-\epsilon^0_{kl}\right)\,d\Omega
\end{equation}

Further, using the definition in Eq.~(3.19), we can define the following mean quantities:

\begin{equation}
S_{ijkl}=\langle \{C_{ijkl}\}\rangle^{-1},\quad
\langle\{\sigma^{\star}_{ij}\}\rangle=\langle \{C_{ijkl}\epsilon^{\star}_{kl}\}\rangle,\quad
\langle\{\sigma^0_{ij}\}\rangle=\langle \{C_{ijkl}\epsilon^0_{kl}\}\rangle
\end{equation}

Using the Eq.~(3.23) and Eq.~(3.24) we obtain

\begin{equation}
E_{ij}=S_{ijkl}[\sigma^A_{kl}+\langle\{\sigma^0_{kl}\}\rangle-\langle\{\sigma^{\star}_{kl}\}\rangle]
\end{equation}

We may restate the elasticity problem as follows: given a non-periodic composition field $c$ on $\Omega$, solve the equation of mechanical equilibrium
\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial}{\partial r_j}\left[C_{ijkl}\left(E_{kl}+e^{\star}_{kl}-e^0_{kl}\right)\right]=0 \quad\text{on } \Omega
\end{equation}

with the constraint

\begin{equation}
E_{ij}=S_{ijkl}\left(\sigma^A_{kl}+\langle\{\sigma^0_{kl}\}\rangle-\langle\{\sigma^{\star}_{kl}\}\rangle\right)
\end{equation}

and the boundary condition

\begin{equation}
e^{\star}_{kl}\text{ is non-periodic on } \Omega.
\end{equation}

Substituting for $C_{ijkl}$ and $e^0_{kl}$ in terms of composition, and $e^{\star}_{kl}$ in terms of the displacement field in the equilibrium equation Eq.~(3.26), and using the symmetry properties of the elastic constants and strains, we obtain
\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial}{\partial r_j}\left[\left(C_{ijkl}^{eff}+\phi(c)\Delta C_{ijkl}\right)\left(E_{kl}+\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{\partial u^{\star}_k}{\partial r_l}+\frac{\partial u^{\star}_l}{\partial r_k}\right)-\beta(c)\epsilon_T\delta_{kl}\right)\right]=0.
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\begin{split}
\left[ C_{ijkl}^{eff}\frac{\partial^2}{\partial r_j \partial r_k} + \Delta C_{ijkl}\frac{\partial}{\partial r_j}\left(\phi(c)\frac{\partial}{\partial r_k}\right) \right] u^{\star}_l(r)
&\quad =C_{ijkl}^{eff}\epsilon_T\delta_{kl}\frac{\partial\beta(c)}{\partial r_j} - \Delta C_{ijkl}E_{kl}\frac{\partial\phi(c)}{\partial r_j} \\
&\quad + \Delta C_{ijkl}^{eff}\epsilon_T\delta_{kl}\frac{\partial\{\phi(c)\beta(c)\}}{\partial r_j}.
\end{split}
\end{equation}
In earlier work on the role of elastic inhomogeneity~[33,19], the homogeneous strain $E_{ij}$ was assumed to take a constant value. For example, in Ref.~[33] it is assumed to be zero, while in Ref.~[19], where microstructural evolution is studied under an applied stress, a constant strain is imposed on the system through a constant $E_{ij}$ tensor. In contrast, our present formulation allows for a prescribed overall stress (which is achieved by controlling the strain). This approach is known as ``stress-control based on strain-control'' (see Appendix D of Ref.~[31]).
\subsection{Computational algorithm}
The equation of mechanical equilibrium, Eq.~(3.30), can be solved numerically using an iterative solver such as the Liebmann method.We begin with the zeroth order approximation to the solution by assuming that $\Delta C_{ijkl} = 0$. Thus, Equation reduces to:
\begin{equation}
C_{ijkl}^{eff}\frac{\partial^2 u^{\star}_l}{\partial r_j \partial r_k} = C_{ijkl}^{eff}\epsilon_T\delta_{kl}\frac{\partial\beta(c)}{\partial r_j}
\end{equation}
Taking $\epsilon_{ij}^T=C_{ijkl}^{eff}\epsilon_T\delta_{kl}$, we can write the above equation as:
\begin{equation}
C_{ijkl}^{eff}\frac{\partial^2 u^{\star}_l}{\partial r_j \partial r_k} = \epsilon_{ij}^T\frac{\partial\beta(c)}{\partial r_j}
\end{equation}
For i=1,2 above equation will form as two linear equations in $u_x$ and $u_y$. We can solve these equations using the Liebmann method. After solving for $u_x$ and $u_y$, we can calculate the elastic strain and stress fields using Eq.~(3.29) and Eq.~(3.13), respectively. We can then calculate the elastic contribution to the chemical potential $\mu^{el}$ by taking the functional derivative of $F^{el}$ with respect to composition. Finally, we can substitute $\mu^{el}$ into the Cahn-Hilliard equation to get the final form of the Cahn-Hilliard equation with elasticity, which can be solved using a finite difference method.
\section{Simulation Algorithm}

\subsection{Spatial Discretization}

We discretize the spatial domain using a uniform grid with spacing $\Delta x$ and $\Delta y$ in the $x$ and $y$ directions, respectively. The composition field $c(x,y,t)$ is approximated at grid points $(i\Delta x, j\Delta y)$ as $c_{i,j}^n$, where $n$ denotes the time step. The Laplacian operator is discretized using central differences:

\begin{equation}\label{eq:fd_laplacian}
\nabla^2 c_{i,j} \approx \frac{c_{i+1,j} - 2c_{i,j} + c_{i-1,j}}{(\Delta x)^2} + \frac{c_{i,j+1} - 2c_{i,j} + c_{i,j-1}}{(\Delta y)^2},
\end{equation}

and similarly for other field variables. For the chemical potential, we have
\begin{equation}
\mu_{i,j} = h(c_{i,j}) - 2K_c \nabla^2 c_{i,j} + \mu^{el}_{i,j},
\end{equation}
where $h(c_{i,j}) = \partial f_0/\partial c|_{c_{i,j}}$ is the local chemical potential, and $\mu^{el}_{i,j}$ is the elastic contribution at grid point $(i,j)$.

\subsection{Temporal Discretization of Cahn-Hilliard Equation}

The Cahn--Hilliard equation (Eq.~(3.1)) is discretized in time using a semi-implicit scheme, where the nonlinear term $h(c)$ is treated explicitly and the fourth-order derivative term is treated implicitly:

\begin{equation}\label{eq:ch_semi_implicit}
\frac{c_{i,j}^{n+1} - c_{i,j}^{n}}{\Delta t} = M \nabla^2 \mu_{i,j}^{\star},
\end{equation}

where $\mu_{i,j}^{\star}$ is a stabilized chemical potential. To ensure numerical stability, we use:
\begin{equation}
\mu_{i,j}^{\star} = h(c_{i,j}^{n}) - 2K_c \nabla^2 c_{i,j}^{n+1} + \mu_{i,j}^{el,n}
\end{equation}

Expanding and rearranging, the discretized update formula becomes:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ch_update}
c_{i,j}^{n+1} - M\Delta t \cdot 2K_c \nabla^4 c_{i,j}^{n+1} = c_{i,j}^{n} + M\Delta t \nabla^2\left(h(c_{i,j}^{n}) + \mu_{i,j}^{el,n}\right),
\end{equation}

where $\nabla^4 = \nabla^2(\nabla^2)$ is the biharmonic operator. This is a linear system in $c^{n+1}$ that can be solved iteratively at each time step.

\subsection{Temporal Discretization of Allen-Cahn Equation}

The Allen--Cahn equation (Eq.~(3.2)) is discretized explicitly in time:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ac_fd}
\frac{\eta_{i,j}^{n+1} - \eta_{i,j}^{n}}{\Delta t} = -L\left[\frac{\delta (F^{ch}/N_v)}{\delta \eta}\bigg|_{i,j}^{n} + \frac{\delta (F^{el}/N_v)}{\delta \eta}\bigg|_{i,j}^{n}\right].
\end{equation}

where $\delta F^{ch}/\delta\eta$ includes the double well and gradient terms:
\begin{equation}
\frac{\delta (F^{ch}/N_v)}{\delta \eta} = \frac{\partial f(c,\eta)}{\partial \eta} - 2K_{\eta}\nabla^2\eta.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Mechanical Equilibrium Solver}

At each time step, the mechanical equilibrium equation (Eq.~(3.30)) is solved using the iterative Liebmann method. The displacement field $u^{\star}_{l,i,j}$ is updated at each iteration using:

\begin{equation}\label{eq:mech_eq_fd}
u_{l,i,j}^{\star,(k+1)} = u_{l,i,j}^{\star,(k)} + \omega \cdot \text{Residual}^{(k)}_{i,j}
\end{equation}

where $\omega$ is a relaxation parameter (typically $\omega \in [0.5, 1.5]$), $\text{Residual}^{(k)}_{i,j}$ is the residual from Eq.~(3.30) computed using Eq.~\eqref{eq:fd_laplacian}, and $k$ denotes the iteration count within a time step.

\subsection{Numerical Algorithm}

The overall time-stepping algorithm is:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Given $c_{i,j}^n$ and $\eta_{i,j}^n$, compute $h(c_{i,j}^n)$ at all grid points.
\item Assemble the composition-dependent elastic moduli and eigenstrain fields.
\item Solve the mechanical equilibrium (Eq.~(3.30)) iteratively using Liebmann method until $\text{Error} < \text{tol}$ (typically $10^{-8}$), obtaining $u^{\star}_{i,j}$.
\item Compute the elastic strain and stress from the displacement field using Eq.~\eqref{eq:fd_laplacian}.
\item Calculate the elastic chemical potential $\mu^{el}_{i,j}$ by functional derivative of $F^{el}$.
\item Solve the discretized Cahn-Hilliard equation (Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch_update}) for $c_{i,j}^{n+1}$.
\item Update the order parameter using Eq.~\eqref{eq:ac_fd} to obtain $\eta_{i,j}^{n+1}$.
\item Advance to time step $n+1$ and repeat.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Convergence and Error Control}

Convergence of the mechanical equilibrium solver is monitored using the discrete $L^2$-norm:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:convergence_norm}
\mathrm{Error}^{(k)} = \left(\sum_{i,j} \big( u^{(k+1)}_{i,j} - u^{(k)}_{i,j} \big)^{2}\right)^{1/2}.
\end{equation}

The solution is considered converged when $\mathrm{Error}^{(k)} < \text{tol}_{\mathrm{mech}}$. Additionally, to enhance stability and reduce computation, from the second time step onwards the displacement solution from the previous time step is used as the initial guess for the Liebmann iterations.
